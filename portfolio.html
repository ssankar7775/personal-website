MRacing Formula SAE

As a Formula 1 enthusiast, I eagerly joined the MRacing project team during my second semester at the University of Michigan. I joined the Aerodynamics team, where I acquired the foundational knowledge of aerodynamic analysis and comprehended the pivotal role of downforce in optimizing the controllability of formula cars during turns. Michigan Vertical Flight Technology (MVFT), Model Based Systems Engineering

Fall 2022

As a part of the inaugural Model-Based Systems Engineering course at the University of Michigan, I acquired the knowledge and skills necessary to effectively apply MBSE principles to an external project team, the Michigan Vertical Flight Technology team. This team was entrusted with the objective of designing, constructing, and operating an electric Vertical Take Off and Landing (eVTOL) vehicle for the Vertical Flight Society’s annual competition. As a Model-Based Systems Engineer for this team, my responsibilities included assisting the team in developing a comprehensive concept of operations, meticulously defining detailed requirements, and subsequently creating Operational Analysis, System Analysis, Logical Architecture, and Physical Architecture utilizing Capella, Cameo, and SysML. Additionally, we conducted Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) analysis on our propeller designs to ascertain the optimal propeller design for our preowned motors derived from previous flights.

In collaboration with the MBSE team, I participated in a Systems Requirements Review (SRR) with esteemed industry representatives from Lockheed Martin, Blue Origin, General Atomics, Boeing, and our team sponsors. Subsequently, the team conducted a Preliminary Design Review (PDR) with the same industry representatives and team sponsors, during which the team’s designs, modifications, architectures, and verification and validation documentation were meticulously scrutinized. Following this PDR, I assumed a leadership role within the Michigan Aeronautical Science Association (MASA) as the Aerodynamics and Recovery Lead.

If you are interested, please feel free to contact me for further information.

In the summer of 2022, I initially joined the team working with the Aerodynamics and Recovery team, working on a few projects such as the manufacturing of composite structures (fins and avionics/recovery bay) as well as the Separation and Deployment mechanism, which I thereafter took ownership of. I also worked with the Assembly Test and Launch Operation (ATLO) Team, where I gained valuable experiences in rocket plumbing by learning how to interpret P&IDs and operate with solenoids, Swagelok, NPT, and AN fittings. With the ATLO team, I also participated in Coldflow campaigns, conducting leak checks and ensuring that plumbing was properly cleaned and stored safely.

Following the summer of working with the recovery, composites, and fins team, I was named project lead of all three projects and continued the development of each project until launch integration. After 3 months of being project lead, I was named the Aerodynamics and Recovery lead for the last 6 months of the Clementine project where I oversaw 40 people among 11 different projects until launch integration. Following the launch of Clementine, I was the Marketing lead for three months after which I had to reevaluate my priorities and focus on my academic pursuits.

Clementine's Seperation and Deployment Mechanism

As part of the Aerodynamics and Recovery team, I joined as the team conducted tests on the separation and deployment mechanism designed to separate the nosecone from the rocket body and deploy the recovery parachutes. The initial system consisted of a one-foot piston with a 1” diameter, actuated by a pyrotechnic bolt at its base. The initial tests involved fitting a 1/8” bolt with a piece of shrink wrap filled with black powder and an ematch. The primary objective was to determine the amount of black powder required to actuate the piston and displace 30 lbf. However, inconsistencies arose due to the shrink wrap clogging the opening caused by the heat generated by the black powder. In response to the need for system improvement, I assumed ownership of the combustible paper housing for black powder and developed comprehensive standard operating procedures for pyrotechnic manufacturing and testing. Additionally, I introduced a black powder training program, which was approved by the facility’s Environmental Health and Safety (EHS) Officer.

Following the implementation of these protocols, I became the project lead for the Separation and Deployment mechanism. Despite various iterations of plumbing and safety enhancements, the pyrotechnic mechanism continued to exhibit inconsistencies and unreliability.

Under pressure from the impending deadline and the rest of the team, I embarked on a self-directed design project for a pneumatic system. Within two weeks, I presented a conceptual demonstration to the team. Although the team expressed reservations about adopting a new design so close to the deadline, I convened an emergency design review to ensure everyone’s participation in setting up the pneumatic system.

The system was designed with a NPT T-fitting from the piston, featuring an outlet and relief valve on one end and a solenoid attachment on the other. An accumulator was manufactured by the ATLO team due to the expedited timeline of the mechanism and was positioned upstream of the solenoid. The accumulator pressure was designed to maintain a maximum of 1100 psi with a safety factor of 1.3, considering size and mass constraints.

The system underwent rigorous testing and proved successful in all component and system evaluations. Regrettably, an avionics battery fire occurred shortly after the rocket’s launch from the launch rail, resulting in the loss of telemetry and the inability to activate the recovery mechanism’s solenoid, triggering the deployment of the parachutes.

Human Powered Submarine

Fall 2023 - Summer 2024

During my senior year, I joined the Human Powered Submarine (HPS) team with the intention of providing advisory support to younger students in assembling a team with strong engineering principles while prioritizing my academic pursuits. The HPS team is dedicated to constructing a flooded submarine capable of navigating intricate obstacle courses and speed races through the utilization of the pilot’s power. The submarine is entered into the European International Submarine Race (eISR) and the International Submarine Race (ISR) during alternate years. This year, the team embarked on a project to design, construct, and manufacture a new submarine named Marlin to compete in eISR. The team comprised 15 students, including five non-engineer divers and only four upperclassmen with prior engineering experience.

Given the existing leadership within the team, I joined to impart knowledge on utilizing CAD software, specifically SolidWorks and NX, and guide the team’s transition from Dassault System’s software to Siemens. This transition enabled the team to access the University’s teamcenter license for sharing large files. In addition to software instruction, I also imparted guidance on proper General Dimensioning and Tolerance (GD&T) standards and first principles engineering.

After several months, I recognized my ability to contribute more effectively to the team and assumed a smaller project. This project involved designing, manufacturing, and constructing a tensioner system for the drive train, ensuring its accessibility to the divers. During this process of manufacturing the tensioner, I trained a younger student on using a mill and gained experience on lathes.

CubeSat Flight Lab

Fall 2023 - Summer 2024

The CubeSat Flight Lab (CFL) offers a research-based course that equips students with knowledge and skills to develop CubeSat missions. After my tenure at MASA, I was particularly drawn to the CFL’s capstone class, which allows students to earn credit while collaborating in a team-oriented environment.

The 495-level class is a one-semester program that immerses students in the comprehensive Systems V design cycle, culminating in a StratoSat launch aboard a High-Altitude Balloon. The students learn about spacecraft development through the Space Mission Analysis and Design textbook and apply the concepts throughout the semester to design, build, test, and launch a StratoSat.

Following the 495-level class, students have the option to delve deeper into CubeSat development in a 700-level research-based class. This class prepares students for Michigan’s next CubeSat launch, Michigan Cubed - 10 (MC-10). During the conceptual phase of the project, which involved securing funding for the CubeSat’s construction and submitting a NASA CSLI proposal to identify a cost-effective path to Low Earth Orbit (LEO), students utilized this semester to establish a system architecture and circuitry foundation for payloads developed by other laboratories at the University. These payloads included a magnetometer and an Organic Photovoltaic cell. These payloads were integrated into a StratoSat to assess the feasibility of such a mission.

495 Capstone Class

Students are divided into teams of five, each assigned specific requirements for their CubeSat mission. During lectures, students gain insights into Space Mission Design and Analysis, while outside class, they design, construct, test, and launch their team’s StratoSat.

Each team adopts a unique approach, with each member assuming ownership of a subsystem. I served as the Systems Engineer for our StratoSat payload, which included two PNI RM3100 sensors and a Raspberry Pi camera. The system also included a Raspberry Pi 4B+ flight computer, a Pluto Software Defined Radio, a Power Distribution Unit (PDU) designed by a previous student, and various sensors. These components were housed within a 3D printed PLA structure. As Systems Engineer, I refined requirements, drafted concept of operations, verification and validation documents, and testing procedures. I introduced a ground plane beneath GPS antenna during testing, making our StratoSat the sole team to record GPS data. Despite camera malfunction due to memory allocation issues, primary payload data (magnetometers) were successfully recovered.

740 Research Class

The 740 research class, was a student led class of 36 senior undergraduate and graduate students, aimed to design and develop Michigan’s next CubeSat, the MC-10. Two labs at the University of Michigan had developed an Organic Photovoltaic cell and a Magnetometer payloads for the CubeSat. The team, comprising five students in each of six subsystems and a Systems team, aimed to identify a system architecture and demonstrate its functionality through a High-Altitude Balloon flight.

The teams were responsible for command and data handling, communications, structures, electronic power systems, and payload development. The initial architecture was designed to facilitate the development of two minimum viable products (MVPs) within 12 weeks. However, communication challenges hindered the development process. Upon reaching the first MVP deadline, the team faced numerous unmet requirements, leading to a project restructuring. The Systems team implemented measures like eliminating attendance restrictions, weekly design updates, adopting Redmine (open source version of JIRA), and enhancing the system’s team involvement.

To ensure accountability for the HAB launch, the team developed three additional MVPs. However, two weeks before the launch, JPL’s F prime software issues caused software integration and testing complications. A few days before, the team switched to Python and integrated the software overnight.

However, memory allocation issues with the new Python-based CDH during the launch caused a failed mission for the semester. The launch was thoroughly investigated and documented.

Summer 2024 Continued Work

Due to several unforeseen circumstances with my transfer, I was informed the evening before Winter graduation that I was two credits short of meeting the graduation requirements. To rectify this situation, I dedicated the summer to collaborating with the Canadian Forces Leadership Institute (CFL) in the development of a Flight Termination Unit (FTU) for high-altitude balloon flights. During this project, I integrated an RP2040-based microcontroller with a LoRa bonnet developed by Adafruit and designed a system architecture for a Nichrome-based termination unit. While this project was initially presented at the concept demonstration phase, it has now been taken over by a new team.

Additionally, I served as a systems engineer for the planned StratoSat named SL-1. This mission aimed to provide the team with operational visibility (OPV) and magnetometer data that was previously unavailable due to a previous failure. Furthermore, it sought to reduce the size of the radio and flight computer by utilizing a smaller payload. The primary objective of this mission was to record payload data at an altitude of 35 km, which would result in a significant reduction in mass from the previous 7 kg StratoSat to a 1.2 kg StratoSat. Regrettably, due to unfavorable launch conditions and a shortage of personnel, the mission was ultimately canceled.
